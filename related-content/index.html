Always Link Your Headings

  • Avoid linking "Learn More" text. This provides no context to assistive technology users who navigate by hyperlink (and is bad for SEO). Instead, link your title and use the pseudo-element trick to make entire region clickable.
  • This being a well-known and ubiquitous pattern on the web, ask yourself if "Learn More" is even required here.
  • Approach linking of entire region (image, title, description) with extreme caution. This only creates extra "noise" for assistive technology users. Being concise here makes for a more pleasant experience.
  • If images are present, use alt text when needed. Do not repeat the title text in alt text.
  • Make sure your heading levels are based on surrounding content. These tend to be h3 or h2. Skipping of headings should be avoided.
  • If content is not present (or removed in the future), a heading must not be used.
  • Be mindful of focus states. Applying :focus to li or div will not work. Use :focus-within instead.

No Content Means No Heading

  • When a related content area has no content other than a title—do not use a heading.
  • Headings must only be used to precede content (paragraphs, lists, etc.)
  • If asked to remove content, the heading must be changed to a div or span.
  • Heading adjustments are a common task in accessibility, so using classes on your headings makes future mark-up changes easier.
  • The pseudo-element trick can be used here as well.
